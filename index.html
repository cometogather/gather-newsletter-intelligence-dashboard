Key insights

Insight 01
The program runs at 8.8× the LinkedIn benchmark — consistently
Across all six issues, the floor CTR was 2.14% and the ceiling was 6.73%. The LinkedIn average for sponsored content is 0.55%. Even the weakest issue ran at nearly 4× benchmark — which means the editorial quality is carrying the distribution.
Issue 1 (lowest): 2.14% CTR. Issue 5 (peak): 6.73% CTR. LinkedIn benchmark: 0.55%. Program floor = 3.9× benchmark.
Insight 02
Named-account targeting outperforms general reach when the frame is concrete
In three of five dual-campaign issues, the Target Account audience outperformed General Audience — and every time it did, the editorial frame was specific and operational, not broadly aspirational. Abstract thought-leadership content reliably underperformed with named accounts.
Target outperformed GA on Issues 4, 5, and 6. Target 8.02% vs GA 5.94% (Issue 5). Target 5.63% vs GA 4.89% (Issue 6). Target 4.18% vs GA 2.87% (Issue 4).
Insight 03
The first line of the creative determines whether the ad gets clicked
Across the six best-performing unique ad creatives, every one opens with the argument rather than the setup. The posts that lead with a direct claim or a named tension outperform those that build context before delivering the point — often by a factor of 2–3×.
Six openers above 5% CTR (8.02%, 6.44%, 5.94%, 5.63%, 5.12%, 4.71%) all lead with a direct statement. No top-performing creative opened with a question or background framing.
Insight 04
C-suite and board readers are the most CTR-responsive audience segment
CEO, President, and board-level readers consistently produce the highest click rates — particularly on issues that name a leadership tension or a hidden organizational gap. They are substantially less responsive to general AI content and most responsive to frames that name something they're privately navigating.
C-Suite peaks: 9.14% (Issue 5), 6.20% (Issue 3), 5.88% (Issue 6). Lowest C-suite CTR: 1.84% (Issue 1 — the program's introductory, non-provocation issue).
Insight 05
Every issue's performance informs the next issue's creative strategy
The program's CTR arc shows a clear learning curve: as creative targeting and editorial voice sharpened across issues, average performance rose. Issue 5 (the program peak) directly applied audience-signal learnings from Issues 3 and 4. The data compounds.
Program avg Issues 1–3: 4.31% CTR. Program avg Issues 4–6: 5.10% CTR. 18% improvement in average CTR across the second half of the program.
Insight 06
The editorial program is generating pipeline intelligence, not just impressions
CTR data segmented by company, title, and function is commercially actionable. Which named accounts clicked most on which themes tells you what each account is paying attention to — that's a call context, not just a content metric. Issues 5 and 3 generated the clearest signal on specific target accounts.
BlackRock: 11.42% CTR (Issue 5). Ascension Health: 9.33% (Issue 3). McKinsey & Company: 8.17% (Issue 3). Each represents a specific company whose senior execs clicked through on a specific editorial frame.

Audience field notes

Enterprise Technology Accounts

Who: Large platform and enterprise software companies (IBM, Salesforce, Microsoft, SAP, ServiceNow, Oracle)
Pattern: Over-index on organizational and structural themes — not on AI-utility content. Respond to editorial that addresses the gap between technical systems and the people and processes around them. Issue 3 (structural misalignment) and Issue 6 (adaptive capacity) produced the strongest cross-account signal.
Evidence: Salesforce 7.83% (Issue 2). Microsoft 7.31% (Issue 5). SAP 5.94% (Issue 4). Target campaign outperformed GA on Issues 4, 5, and 6 — all with concrete, non-aspirational frames.

Financial Services Firms

Who: Global banks, asset managers, and investment firms (BlackRock, JPMorgan Chase, KKR, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Wells Fargo)
Pattern: Peaked sharply on Issue 5 (operational intelligence) — the program's highest-CTR issue overall. Finance leaders respond to content that is analytically grounded and commercially specific. They engage selectively; when they do engage, the CTRs are exceptionally high.
Evidence: BlackRock 11.42% (Issue 5). KKR 9.87% (Issue 5). JPMorgan Chase 8.54% (Issue 5). Finance function CTR on Issue 5: 9.88% — the highest function CTR across the program.

Healthcare & Professional Services

Who: Health systems, consulting firms, and advisory organizations (Ascension Health, McKinsey & Company, Accenture, Deloitte, Intermountain Health, Kaiser Permanente)
Pattern: Lean into organizational design and change-readiness themes. Issue 3 produced the highest account-level CTRs for this segment. They engage most when content names a gap between intent and execution — structural, cultural, or strategic.
Evidence: Ascension Health 9.33% (Issue 3), 4.93% (Issue 6). McKinsey & Company 8.17% (Issue 3), 4.72% (Issue 2). Consulting function CTR: 8.44% (Issue 3), 7.93% (Issue 5).

Named Target Accounts

Who: The directly targeted prospect list — organizations approached via the Target Account campaign layer
Pattern: When the content frame is concrete and operationally specific, Target accounts outperform General Audience by a meaningful margin. Three of five dual-campaign issues saw Target > GA. When the frame is broad or aspirational, the gap closes or reverses.
Evidence: Target 8.02% vs GA 5.94% (Issue 5). Target 5.63% vs GA 4.89% (Issue 6). Target 4.18% vs GA 2.87% (Issue 4). GA outperformed Target on Issues 2 and 3 — both issues with broader, less account-specific frames.

Pipeline playbook

Three ways to turn this data into pipeline conversations

The data above is not just a content report — it's a map of what your target accounts are paying attention to, and when. Here is how to convert it into active pipeline development. • Cross-reference the top-account CTR data against your open pipeline. Any named account that clicked through at >7% CTR on Issue 5 or Issue 3 has self-selected into the exact conversation you want to have. That's not a cold call — it's a warm continuation. Use the issue theme as the opening frame. • Use high-performing editorial themes as BD sequencing inputs. Issues 3 ("When the structure can't hold the strategy") and Issue 5 ("The signal hiding inside your operations") generated the strongest signal from C-suite and Finance leaders. Those frames — organizational misalignment, operational intelligence gaps — are the conversations those buyers are already having internally. Sequence outreach around them. • Build the next editorial cycle around the themes with the highest Target Account CTR. Issues where Target > GA identify frames that resonate specifically with named prospects, not just broad audiences. Issues 4, 5, and 6 all showed this pattern. The next three issues should go deeper on those themes — and the targeting should become more precise, not broader.
